Confusion with stochastic o-notation in M-estimator consistency proof from van der Vaart

2020-07-08

From Asymptotic Statistics by A.W. van der Vaart pg 45-46.

Theorem

Let \(M_n\) be random functions and let \(M\) be a non-random function of \(\theta\) such that

\[\begin{gather}\sup_\theta |M_n(\theta) - M(\theta)| \to^p 0 \\ \sup_{\theta: d(\hat{\theta}_n, \theta)\geq \epsilon}\quad M(\theta) < M(\theta_0) \end{gather}\]

Then if a sequence of estimators \(\hat{\theta}_n\) obeys

\[M_n(\hat{\theta}_n) \geq M_n(\theta_0) - o_p(1)\]

Then \(\hat{\theta}_n\) is consistent for \(\theta_0\).

What I was confused by

In the proof the following step is made

\[\begin{align}M_n(\hat{\theta}_n) & \geq M_n(\theta_0) - o_p(1)\\ M_n(\hat{\theta}_n) &\geq M(\theta_0)-o_p(1) \end{align}\]

and this is justified by saying:

Because uniform convergence of \(M_n\) to \(M\) implies convergence of \(M_n(\theta_0) \to^p M(\theta_0)\), the right side equals \(M(\theta_0) - o_P(1)\). It follows that \(M_n(\hat{\theta}_n) \geq M(\theta_0) - o_P(1)\) […]

I thought this was saying that you directly take the limit of just the \(M_n(\theta_0)\) term and saying it converges to \(M(\theta_0)\) which is a weird thing to think:

  • you can’t take the limit of just one side
  • even if you could, you can’t guarantee that \(M_n(\theta_0)\geq M_{n+1}(\theta_0) -o_P(1)\) ASSUMING you have the same sequence of \(o_P(1)\)

Instead I needed to realize that from a higher level, the \(o_P(1)\) from \(M_n(\hat{\theta}_n) \geq M_n(\theta_0) - o_P(1)\) and the \(o_P(1)\) from \(M_n(\hat{\theta}_n) \geq M(\theta_0)-o_P(1)\) are different sequences!

One way to see this by noting what is and is not random anymore. Since \(M_n(\hat{\theta}_n)\) is still the same term it’s still random. But \(M(\theta)\) is fixed (by assumption). So the random-ness of \(M_n(\theta_0)\) had to be absorbed by the \(O_P(1)\) term.

The second way would be to try to force the terms as needed:

\[\begin{align}M_n(\hat{\theta}_n) &\geq M_n(\theta_0) - O_P(1)\\ & = M_n(\theta_0) - M(\theta_0) + M(\theta_0) - o_P(1) \\ & = o_P(1) + M(\theta_0) - o_P(1)\end{align} \]

We can then absorbe the first \(o_P(1)\) into the second.
