--- title: O notation depends on what limit you're taking author: Stone date: '2020-07-02' categories: - math slug: o-notation-depends-on-what-limit-you-re-taking ---

Something that confused me about \(O\) notation was that it appeared to take different meaning between algorithms and calculus. This was resolved by seeing that the notation provides a convenient way of describing the limit of a function \(f(x)\) as \(x\) approaches a specific limit. This point about the \(O\)-ness of a function being contingent on what limit you were taking was the crux of my confusion.

This limit is typically different between a standard algorithms and calculus course. In algorithms \(x \to \infty\), while in calculus \(x \to 0\).

For example we have in algorithms:

\[x^2 + x = O(x^2)\quad x \to \infty\]

since

\[\lim_{x\to\infty} \frac{x^2 + x}{x^2} = 1\]

but in calculus we would ask \(x \to 0\) (typically) so:

\[\lim_{x\to 0} \frac{x^2 + x}{x^2} = 1 + \frac{1}{x}\]

which diverges.
